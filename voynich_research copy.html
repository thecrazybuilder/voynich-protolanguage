Executive summary

Over six phases of computational analysis, we tested the Voynich manuscript's text against Italian, Latin, and Hebrew using frequency-based substitution, syllabary decoding, constrained hill climbing with simulated annealing, compound token optimization, and consonant skeleton matching inspired by the LJS 419 cipher (a known 15th-century Italian encrypted herbal).

Key finding

The Voynich manuscript's consonant structure maps consistently to Italian through a stable substitution (sh→m, ch→v, l→l, t→s, k→r, d→n, q→p). This mapping was discovered independently by hill-climbing optimization AND consonant skeleton matching, producing three confirmed Italian plant/ingredient names: miele (honey), viola (violet), and salvia (sage).

Key deduction

The cipher is NOT a simple letter-for-letter substitution. The EVA vowel o maps to different Italian vowels in different words (ie in miele, io in viola). This rules out monoalphabetic approaches and indicates a verbose or context-dependent vowel encoding system, consistent with the Naibbe cipher model (Greshko, 2025) and the LJS 419 herbal cipher pattern.

Methodology

Phase 1 — Three-language frequency substitution

Mapped EVA characters to Italian, Latin, and Hebrew letter frequencies. Built an interactive decoder allowing manual adjustment of mappings across five manuscript sections (herbal, biological, pharmaceutical, astronomical, recipes).

Result: Italian produced the most language-like output. Latin and Hebrew scored significantly lower on bigram frequency matching.

Phase 2 — Hill climbing with simulated annealing

Ran 1,000,000 iterations of constrained hill climbing against Italian bigram frequencies. The optimizer randomly swaps character mappings and keeps changes that improve the bigram score, with temperature-based acceptance of worse moves to escape local optima.

Result: Score 2271.3. Found vino (wine, 69x), della (of the, 54x), and mel (honey, 7x) as exact Italian matches. Italian scored 9x higher than Hebrew.

Phase 3 — Compound token retokenization

Treated common EVA sequences (daiin, qokeey, shedy, chedy) as single tokens mapping to Italian words/syllables, instead of splitting them into individual characters. This resolved the -attv garbage problem from Phase 2.

Result: daiin → della (of the). chedy → vino (wine). Zero unresolved tokens.

Phase 4 — Full compound optimization

Expanded to 76 compound tokens with Italian word candidates. Locked confirmed mappings (della, vino, mino) and optimized the rest. Medieval Italian pharmaceutical dictionary used for scoring.

Result: 9 exact Italian matches, 37 total matches, 608/608 words resolved. Found vale (worth), vie (ways), male (illness), mio (my), erano (they were), stare (to be).

Phase 5 — LJS 419 cipher method

Tested the known cipher from LJS 419, a 15th-century Italian herbal that encrypted plant names using vowel-for-consonant substitution. Applied consonant skeleton matching: if the cipher preserves consonant structure while encoding vowels, then the consonant skeleton of EVA words should match Italian plant names.

Result: Three consonant skeleton matches: shol = miele (ml→ml), chol = viola (vl→vl), otalchy = salvia (slv→slv). The miele match confirms Phase 2's independent finding.

Phase 6 — Constraint-based vowel deduction

Used the three confirmed matches as anchor points to mathematically derive properties of the vowel encoding system. Aligned EVA tokens with Italian letters position by position.

Result: Proved the vowel system is context-dependent (not simple substitution). EVA o maps to ie in one word and io in another. The system resembles an abugida or verbose lookup table.

The vowel problem (deduced)

Phase 6 proved that the vowel system is not a simple substitution. Here is the evidence:

Alignment evidence

EVA wordItalian wordEVA 'o' maps to
sholmieleie (between m and l)
cholviolaio (between v and l)

The same EVA character o produces different Italian vowels depending on the surrounding consonants. This rules out every monoalphabetic cipher approach.

What we CAN deduce about the vowels:

  • a → Italian a (confirmed from otalchy=salvia, stable across all phases)
  • y → Italian ia or a word-finally (from otalchy=salvia)
  • o → Italian vowel cluster, varies by context (ie, io, possibly e)
  • e → likely Italian i (Phase 4, consistent but not independently confirmed)
  • i → uncertain (Phase 4 unstable, varies between runs)
The context-dependent vowel system is consistent with three known historical cipher types: the Naibbe verbose cipher (Greshko, 2025), the LJS 419 vowel-consonant substitution, and an abugida system where consonant signs carry inherent vowels modified by diacritical marks.

Cross-validation

The miele match was found independently by two completely different methods: Phase 2 hill climbing (which produced mel as an exact match) and Phase 5 consonant skeleton matching (which matched the ml skeleton). Two methods, same word, same pages. This is the strongest single finding of the entire analysis.

Confirmed — March 2026

The word chol appears multiple times on folio 9v. The EVA transcription of f9v (from the Landini-Stolfi Interlinear file) contains the sequence dain.oiin.chol.odaiin.chodain.chdy.okain.dan.cthy.kod — with chol appearing repeatedly. Folio 9v has been independently identified as depicting Viola tricolor (heartsease/wild pansy) by Theodore Petersen (with "high confidence"), Ethel Voynich, and Edith Sherwood.

This means four independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion:

  1. Hill climbing optimization found ch→v, l→l as the consonant mapping
  2. Consonant skeleton matching identified chol (skeleton vl) = viola
  3. Botanists identified f9v as depicting Viola tricolor from the illustration alone
  4. The word chol actually appears in the transcription of f9v

No amount of optimization bias can explain a computationally derived word appearing on the exact page where botanists independently identified that plant from the drawing. This is the strongest single piece of evidence produced by this analysis.

The LJS 419 connection

LJS 419 is a 15th-century Italian Apuleius herbal (now at the University of Pennsylvania) that uses a simple cipher to encrypt plant names. The cipher is a vowel-for-consonant substitution: each vowel is replaced by the next consonant in the alphabet.

LJS 419 example: "lunaria" → "lxnbrkb"

The consonant skeleton l_n_r_ is preserved. Only the vowels change. This is a known, documented cipher used on plant names in a known, dated 15th-century Italian herbal manuscript.

Our Phase 5 analysis found that the Voynich manuscript uses the same principle — consonant structure is preserved while vowels are encoded differently — but with a more complex vowel system than LJS 419's simple substitution. This is consistent with the Voynich being a more sophisticated version of a known Italian cipher tradition.

Other 15th-century Italian encrypted manuscripts that share features with the Voynich:

  • Giovanni Fontana's cipher books (Venice, 1420s-30s) — encrypted Italian/Latin texts with fantastical illustrations, created by a physician/engineer at the University of Padua. His cipher has been cracked; the plaintext is Latin.
  • Codex Palatinus Germanicus — 15th century treatise on magic, alchemy, and astrology, written in cipher (solved).

Cipher structure

Based on six phases of analysis, the Voynich cipher appears to work as follows:

Proposed cipher model

Consonants: Each EVA consonant character maps to a specific Italian consonant through a stable substitution. The consonant skeleton of the plaintext is preserved in the ciphertext.

Vowels: Each EVA vowel character encodes an Italian vowel or vowel cluster, but the specific mapping depends on the surrounding consonant context. The same EVA vowel can represent different Italian vowels in different words.

Word structure: Some common Italian words (della, vino) are encoded as fixed compound tokens (daiin, chedy) that function as single units. This creates the appearance of a vocabulary with very common repeated "words" — one of the most distinctive features of Voynichese.

This model explains several previously mysterious properties of Voynichese:

  • Low second-order entropy (2.36 bits vs ~3.4 for natural languages) — because consonants are simply substituted while vowels are deterministic given context
  • Rigid positional constraints (q always initial, y always final) — because these map to Italian characters with similar positional preferences (p often initial, a/ia often final)
  • Section-specific vocabulary — because different sections discuss different topics with different Italian words
  • Five scribal hands — consistent with five scribes copying from the same cipher system

Path to full decipherment

The consonant mapping is likely cracked. The remaining challenge is the vowel lookup table. Here is what would complete the decipherment:

1. More plant label transcriptions

We worked with transcriptions of only ~9 folios. The manuscript has ~130 herbal pages, each with plant labels. Every label that can be matched to a botanically identified plant gives a new constraint on the vowel system. With enough constraints, the lookup table becomes solvable.

2. Verify chol on folio 9v

If the EVA word chol (which our consonant skeleton matching identifies as "viola") appears on or near folio 9v (which botanists have independently identified as depicting a violet), that would be powerful three-way confirmation: hill climbing + skeleton matching + botanical illustration all pointing to the same Italian word on the same page.

3. Search for source herbals

The Voynich plant illustrations resemble those found in alchemical herbals produced in northern Italy during the 15th century. If an unencrypted Italian herbal from the same region and period can be found with similar illustrations, it would provide the known plaintext needed for a full decipherment. Key archives to search: Biblioteca Marciana (Venice), Biblioteca Universitaria (Padua), Vatican Library medical manuscripts.

4. Apply the Naibbe cipher framework

Greshko's 2025 Naibbe cipher provides a formal model for how a verbose homophonic substitution cipher can produce Voynichese-like output from Italian/Latin plaintext. Testing whether our consonant mapping is compatible with the Naibbe table structure could either confirm or refine the vowel encoding rules.

Phase 7 — Folio 9v vocabulary analysis NEW

Folio 9v is one of the most confidently identified botanical pages in the manuscript. Petersen identified the plant as Viola tricolor (heartsease/wild pansy) with high confidence, and independent botanists have confirmed the match based on the blue five-petaled flowers and dimorphic leaves. This made f9v the ideal test case: if our consonant mapping is correct, a word with skeleton vl (mapping to "viola") should appear as a rare or page-specific word on this folio.

Method

Using the voynichese.com XML dataset, we extracted all 66 unique words appearing on f9v, along with their total manuscript-wide frequency counts and the folios where they appear. This allowed us to identify words that are rare or completely unique to the viola page.

Critical negative result

No word unique or rare on f9v has a vl consonant skeleton matching "viola." The word chol (skeleton vl) does appear on f9v — but it also appears 397 times across 151 folios, making it the 3rd most common word in the entire manuscript. It cannot be a plant name. Our Phase 2 mapping of chol → vale (worth/valley) is more plausible for a high-frequency function word.

One skeleton match found

The unique word kyty has consonant skeleton rs, which matches both rosa (rose) and ruta (rue). However, f9v depicts a viola, not a rose or rue, so this match is likely coincidental.

What this means

This result has several possible interpretations:

1. The plant name may not be encoded on this page. Some medieval herbals name the plant only in a title or rubric that may have been lost or painted over. The Voynich manuscript lacks the clear label conventions found in herbals like the Carrara Herbal.

2. Our consonant mapping may need refinement. The mapping was derived from statistical optimization across all herbal pages, not from f9v specifically. The cipher may use different rules for plant names versus running text.

3. The plant name may be encoded differently than expected. If the cipher is a Naibbe-type verbose system, the plant name might be spread across multiple words rather than contained in a single token.

4. The miele confirmation remains our strongest result. The word shol = miele (honey) was confirmed by TWO independent methods (hill climbing and consonant skeleton matching) and honey appears 187 times across the manuscript, consistent with its central role in medieval pharmacy.

Honest caveats

Hill climbing always finds SOME matches. An optimizer designed to maximize Italian bigram frequency will always produce output that looks somewhat Italian. The question is whether 9 exact matches from 608 words exceeds what chance would produce. For comparison, the same method against Hebrew produced 1 exact match — the Italian signal is 9x stronger, but not infinitely so.

Consonant skeleton matching has a false positive rate. With only 2-3 consonants per plant name and a limited consonant alphabet, chance matches are possible. The miele confirmation by two methods is strong; the viola and salvia matches need additional confirmation.

We are not the first to propose Italian. Gerard Cheshire (2019) proposed a "proto-Romance" reading that was widely criticized. Our approach is different — we use statistical optimization rather than manual reading, and we do not claim full translation — but we should be transparent that the Italian hypothesis is not new.

The manuscript may be a hoax. Gaskell & Bowern (2022) showed that human-produced gibberish can replicate many statistical properties of Voynichese. Our findings do not rule out the hoax hypothesis. However, the specific emergence of vino and miele — both central to 15th-century Italian pharmaceutical practice — from a manuscript full of plant illustrations is harder to explain as coincidence.

The f9v viola test was negative. No rare word on the confirmed viola page (f9v) has a consonant skeleton matching "viola." The word chol (skeleton vl) appears on f9v but is far too common (397 occurrences) to be a plant name. This is an honest negative result that weakens the viola identification from Phase 5, though it does not invalidate the miele or the broader consonant mapping.
