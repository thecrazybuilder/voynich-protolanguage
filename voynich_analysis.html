Key findings at a glance

The Voynich text has unusually low second-order entropy (2.36 bits vs ~3.4 for natural languages), meaning adjacent characters are highly predictable. This is closer to Chinese or a verbose cipher than to European languages.

Characters show rigid positional constraints — "q" appears almost exclusively at word beginnings, "y" at endings, "r" and "n" almost never start words. Natural languages rarely show this level of regularity.

The 2025 "Naibbe cipher" study showed a historically plausible cipher can produce these exact statistical properties, while a 2022 study found the text statistically resembles human-produced gibberish more than meaningful text.

Notable patterns

The digraphs "ch" and "sh" behave as single characters and are among the most common. The "gallows" characters (t, k, p, f) are rarer but appear in highly specific positions, often at paragraph starts.

Compared to Latin (which would have a flatter distribution), Voynichese has a steeper Zipf curve for characters, more like a syllabary than an alphabet.

The most common word "daiin" appears 821 times — about 2.2% of all tokens. In English, "the" accounts for ~7%, so Voynich words are more evenly distributed.

Word length distribution
Peak at 4-5 characters. Very few words exceed 8 characters. This tight range is unusual — most natural languages have a wider spread. The clustering suggests either a syllabary or a cipher that produces fixed-length output tokens.

Shannon entropy measures information density. H1 = single character, H2 = character pairs, H3 = triplets. Lower H2 means characters are more predictable from context — the hallmark of Voynichese.

What this means

Voynichese H2 (2.36) is dramatically lower than any natural European language (~3.2-3.5). This means if you know one character, you can predict the next with much higher accuracy than in real languages.

Chinese (pinyin) and Cardan grille output come closest. Random gibberish has very HIGH entropy (unpredictable), ruling out pure randomness. The Naibbe cipher output also matches this range.

Why this matters

In natural languages, most letters can appear anywhere. In Voynichese, positions are almost grammatical — as if characters represent syllable components (onset, nucleus, coda) rather than phonemes. This pattern is consistent with an abugida, a syllabary, or a verbose cipher that assigns characters based on word position.

The manuscript has distinct thematic sections with different statistical profiles. Currier identified two "languages" (A and B) and multiple scribal hands.
